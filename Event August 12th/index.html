Remembering Fred Hampton: The Fire Still Burns (1948-1969)

This week, we honor the birth of Fred Hampton, the revolutionary voice of the people born on August 30, 1948. At just 21, Hampton became chairman of the Illinois chapter of the Black Panther Party and sparked a movement rooted in justice, community, and radical love.
 

FRED HAMPTON

Known for his powerful speeches, unwavering solidarity with oppressed groups, and the creation of the Rainbow Coalition, Hampton’s legacy reminds us that leadership doesn’t always come with age—but with clarity of purpose.
Though assassinated in 1969, his ideas and vision live on in modern calls for equity and liberation. As we reflect on his life, let us recommit to building the kind of world Fred fought for—one where the people always come first.

Explore Revolutionary Theatre: Spotlight on August Wilson’s Work

This week, we turn the spotlight to the monumental legacy of August Wilson, one of the most transformative figures in American theatre. Through his Pittsburgh Cycle—a series of ten plays chronicling the African-American experience in each decade of the 20th century—Wilson reshaped the stage into a powerful space for Black voices, memories, and resistance.

THis work centers Black life in all its complexity, from the spiritual resilience in The Piano Lesson to the working-class struggles of Fences and Jitney. Wilson's plays are not just dramas; they are living histories, rich with poetry, jazz rhythms, and the everyday cadences of Black life.
